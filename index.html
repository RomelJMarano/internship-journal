Section 5

PPST Domain Reflections

Reflections on my developmental experiences across the seven domains of the Philippine Professional Standards for Teachers. Click View Full Reflection on any domain to see detailed evidence, numbered experiences, and embedded documents.

A
Domain 1
Content Knowledge and Pedagogy
Experiences
  • Designed and delivered 10+ lesson plans aligned with the K–12 curriculum across Math, Science, English, Araling Panlipunan, MAPEH, and Values Education.
  • Applied the Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract (CPA) approach in teaching Mathematics to make abstract concepts tangible for young learners.
  • Used inquiry-based strategies in Science lessons (e.g., The Water Cycle) to encourage student-led exploration and discovery.
  • Incorporated interactive grammar games and role-play activities to make language lessons engaging and contextual.
Reflections

Teaching content effectively required me to deeply understand the material first — I could not simply follow the textbook. I learned to anticipate student misconceptions, especially in Math, and to have multiple explanations ready. I realized that pedagogy is not a one-size-fits-all approach: the same concept might need a different entry point depending on the learner. This domain pushed me to become not just a teacher of subjects, but a teacher of students.

View Full Reflection & Evidence
B
Domain 2
Learning Environment
Experiences
  • Arranged classroom seating to promote collaborative learning and ease of movement during group activities.
  • Established classroom routines (entry, transitions, dismissal) that minimized disruptions and maximized learning time.
  • Created a print-rich environment with word walls, learning charts, and student-produced displays.
  • Addressed behavioral concerns using positive reinforcement — praise, sticker charts, and class rewards rather than punitive measures.
Reflections

I underestimated how much the physical and emotional environment shapes learning. In my first weeks, I focused on my lesson content and often overlooked transitions — and that is where most disruptions happened. Observing my cooperating teacher showed me that a well-managed classroom is built over time through consistent routines and genuine relationships. By Week 6, our classroom had a rhythm, and I felt the difference in student focus and participation.

View Full Reflection & Evidence
C
Domain 3
Diversity of Learners
Experiences
  • Identified learners with varying ability levels and adapted instruction through differentiated tasks and tiered activities.
  • Provided additional scaffolding (visual aids, manipulatives, peer support) for struggling learners during Math and English lessons.
  • Used flexible grouping strategies — pairing advanced learners with those needing support in collaborative tasks.
  • Incorporated culturally relevant examples and local contexts (community helpers, folk dances) to make content relatable to all learners.
Reflections

Every child in my class of 20 was unique — different learning speeds, different home situations, different strengths. I had to learn to see each student as an individual, not as "the class." Differentiation was the hardest skill to develop, but also the most rewarding. When a struggling student finally grasped a concept through a different approach I had tried, that moment of understanding made every extra hour of planning worthwhile.

View Full Reflection & Evidence
D
Domain 4
Curriculum and Planning
Experiences
  • Prepared detailed Daily Lesson Plans (DLPs) for each teaching day, aligned to the DepEd K–12 curriculum guides and most essential learning competencies (MELCs).
  • Sequenced lessons to build on prior knowledge — ensuring conceptual continuity across weeks.
  • Integrated cross-curricular connections (e.g., linking Math patterns to Art and Music in MAPEH lessons).
  • Submitted weekly lesson plans for review and incorporated feedback from the cooperating teacher into subsequent plans.
Reflections

Planning a lesson is one thing — planning a curriculum is another. I learned to think beyond the single lesson and consider where students are coming from and where they need to go. Time management within lessons was a consistent challenge; I often planned too much content and had to learn to prioritize depth over breadth. My cooperating teacher's advice — "plan for the learner, not for the subject" — became my guiding principle.

View Full Reflection & Evidence
E
Domain 5
Assessment and Reporting
Experiences
  • Administered formative assessments (oral questioning, exit tickets, seatwork, short quizzes) to monitor student understanding throughout each lesson.
  • Prepared and administered summative assessments aligned to competencies covered during the internship period.
  • Recorded and analyzed student performance data to identify learning gaps and adjust subsequent instruction.
  • Communicated student progress to the cooperating teacher and participated in feedback sessions with learners.
Reflections

Assessment transformed from something I did to students into something I did for students. I learned that the real purpose of assessment is not to grade, but to understand. Watching students' faces during oral questioning told me more about their comprehension than any written test. I also struggled at first with providing meaningful written feedback — I defaulted to "Good job!" — but learned to make feedback specific, actionable, and kind.

View Full Reflection & Evidence
F
Domain 6
Community Linkages and Professional Engagement
Experiences
  • Participated in school-wide activities and flag ceremonies, integrating as a professional member of the school community.
  • Maintained respectful and professional communication with school administrators, fellow teachers, and non-teaching staff.
  • Engaged with parents informally during dismissal time to share student updates and build home-school partnerships.
  • Attended school meetings and submitted all required internship documents (lesson plans, journals, reports) promptly and professionally.
Reflections

Teaching does not happen in isolation. The school is a community, and being a professional member of that community required more than just classroom competence. I learned to navigate professional relationships with grace — to seek guidance without appearing incompetent, to collaborate without overstepping, and to represent Daraga Community College with pride in every interaction. This domain reminded me that education is a shared responsibility between the school and the community it serves.

View Full Reflection & Evidence
G
Domain 7
Personal Growth and Professional Development
Experiences
  • Maintained a weekly reflective journal documenting teaching experiences, challenges, and insights throughout the 360-hour internship.
  • Sought and applied feedback from the cooperating teacher and field supervisor to continuously improve instructional practice.
  • Engaged in self-assessment after each teaching day — identifying what worked, what did not, and why.
  • Read professional literature on effective pedagogy, child development, and assessment to supplement practical experience.
Reflections

This domain is the heart of becoming a professional teacher. Reflective practice was not a requirement to complete — it became a habit of mind. I learned that the best teachers are the ones who never stop learning. Every lesson taught me something about my students, my subject, and myself. I entered this internship as a student; I am leaving as a beginning professional — humbled, inspired, and deeply committed to lifelong growth in this noble profession.

Teaching Philosophy

As a Beginning Teacher

"

I believe that every child comes to school with a story — a unique background, a distinct way of seeing the world, and an inherent capacity to grow. As a beginning teacher, my role is not simply to deliver content, but to create the conditions in which every child feels seen, valued, and capable.

Teaching, for me, begins with relationships. Before a student can truly learn from me, they must first trust me. I am committed to building that trust through consistency, genuine care, and the conviction that I believe in every learner's potential — even when they do not yet believe in it themselves.

I approach every lesson with the question: How will this help my students grow — not just as learners, but as people? I believe in active, meaningful learning — learning that connects to students' lives, that challenges them to think critically, and that empowers them to apply knowledge beyond the walls of the classroom.

I also believe that great teachers are lifelong learners. My three months as a pre-service teacher at Malobago Elementary School Site 2 taught me more than any textbook could. I learned that flexibility, empathy, and reflective practice are as essential as any pedagogical skill. I am still learning — and I always will be.

As I begin my professional journey, I carry with me this conviction: Education is the most powerful act of hope we can offer to a child. I am honored to be a part of that work.

— Emelyn S. Ulanday, Beginning Teacher
